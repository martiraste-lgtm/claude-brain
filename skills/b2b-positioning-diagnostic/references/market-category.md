## Overview

There are three approaches to isolate, target, and win in a market. The choice depends on the competitive landscape and business objectives.

At a high level: you can enter an existing market or create a new one. Within existing markets, you can compete for the whole market or position to win a subsegment.

---

## Head to Head

**Goal**: Become the leader in a market category that already exists in customers' minds. Beat the current leader at their own game.

**How it works**: Customers are already educated about what the solution does and doesn't do. They already understand purchase criteria. You can't change the game — you must win on the existing playing field. Evaluation criteria are already established.

**When to use**:
- You ARE already the market leader (the way prospects define the market works in your favor, and you want to keep it that way — prospects compare alternatives on features you have on top)
- The category is well-defined and understood by buyers, but no strong leader exists yet (e.g., smart glasses in early days)
- You have clear data confirming the market exists in buyers' minds or is emerging rapidly

**Advantages**:
- No need to convince people the category needs to exist
- You benefit from the assumptions the category triggers in prospects' minds
- Existing demand can be captured immediately

**Risks and requirements**:
- If a strong leader exists, you need overwhelming differentiation to displace them
- Even without a clear leader, an uncrowded market won't stay that way for long
- For tech companies, this often means growing user base as fast as possible, which requires significant funding (examples: Figma, Canva, Zillow, Slack)
- **Not recommended for early-stage startups** unless you possess clear differentiation AND plan to raise significant capital

**If you ARE the leader**: Reinforce your position. Strengthen buyer criteria. Reiterate your WHY — why you're the best choice to deliver on those criteria, better than all competitors.

## Big Fish, Small Pond

**Goal**: Carve out a piece of an existing market where the rules are slightly different — just enough to give your product an advantage over the category leader.

**How it works**: You leverage what buyers already know about the general category, but focus attention on the fact that requirements for your subsegment are DIFFERENT and not fully met by the general leader. You don't compete head-on — you change the game for a specific audience.

**Analogy**: Head to Head = trying to beat Coca-Cola in the cola market. Big Fish, Small Pond = selling Cola for dogs.

**When to use (ALL criteria must be met)**:
1. The category is well-defined and there's a clear market leader. People must understand what the category means.
2. There's a well-defined group of customers with a unique UNMET NEED not satisfied by the leader
3. That unmet need is clearly definable and demonstrably important
4. You can satisfy the special need MUCH BETTER than the category leader
5. The subsegment is large enough to meet your short-term business objectives

**Subsegmentation variables**: Industry (banking, legal), geography, specific preferences (gluten-free, certified Microsoft developers), ecosystems (Mac users, slow internet users). The subsegment must be easily identifiable.

**Advantages**:
- Dominating a small segment is easier than attacking a large leader directly
- Easier to target and reach a homogeneous segment (list building, community effects, word of mouth)
- Your value proposition can be much more focused and your distinctive advantage more obvious
- After dominating the subsegment, you can expand to adjacent segments

**Execution requirements**:
1. Educate the subsegment that the generic solution doesn't meet their needs
2. Provide proof points showing a clear VALUE GAP between the leader's generic solution and your specific one
3. Help the subsegment understand WHAT THEY GAIN by satisfying their unmet need (quantify the value)
4. Convince them to switch by demonstrating deep understanding of their pain and full resolution

**Critical risk**: Ensure the unmet need is NOT easy for the market leader to cover. Ideally, your competitive advantage is:
- Hard to copy due to IP or technical architecture
- Something that would DAMAGE the leader's general offering if they tried to address it (e.g., it would be hard for Coca-Cola to make "Cola for dogs" without damaging brand associations built over decades)

**Growth path**: Dominate subsegment → expand to adjacent segments → potentially challenge for broader market over time.

## Create a New Category

**Goal**: Define a new frame of reference in customers' minds and position your product as the leader within it.

**How it works**: Instead of being a "embeddable database for mobile" (leveraging existing category), you become a "fluflommer" — a term customers don't know because you just invented it. You must first make the category meaningful before it can serve as useful context.

**The hardest positioning style because it requires customer EDUCATION:**

1. Customers first need to understand WHY the category should exist
2. Why the problem is UNIQUE
3. Why existing solutions in other categories don't solve it well
4. You need a product that demonstrably IS new and different
5. You must counter competitors who will argue you're just a "feature" not a real product
6. You must answer "WHY NOW?" — what factors have made this category possible and/or necessary

**Creating a new category is primarily about selling the PROBLEM, not your solution.** If customers don't understand the potential value of solving the problem, they won't switch from existing solutions.

**When it makes sense**:
- New technology emerges that enables fundamentally different approaches
- Combining existing markets creates one with different buying criteria
- You have a strong differentiator and value proposition that doesn't fit existing categories
- Major economic, political, or technological change creates new needs
- Your solution requires a new way of thinking about category boundaries and purchase criteria
- The company is large enough to capture attention of customers, media, analysts

**When it does NOT make sense (especially for startups)**:
- History shows companies that CREATE categories often LOSE to companies that enter after the hard work is done (Google vs Ask Jeeves, Facebook vs Myspace)
- 90% of tech companies that went public in the last 5 years positioned in EXISTING markets
- Many famous "category creators" actually started in existing categories and expanded later (Salesforce was a niche CRM play until hundreds of millions in revenue; Qualtrics was in the survey software market until hundreds of millions)
- Startups rarely have the resources for the sustained education effort required
- A startup should only choose this if there is truly NO existing category to leverage

**Long-term defense requirement**: Once the category is established and the problem is evident, competitors with more resources and bigger brands will try to take over. Plan for this.

**Triggers for creating a new category**:
- New technology
- Combining existing markets into one with different buying criteria
- Strong differentiator + strong value
- Major disruption (economic, political, technological)
- Or all of these together

## Decision Criteria

Use this decision tree:

**Q1: Does an existing market category describe what you do in a way buyers understand?**
- YES → Go to Q2
- NO → Consider "Create a New Category" (but be very cautious if you're a startup)

**Q2: Is there a dominant leader in that category?**
- NO → Consider "Head to Head" (race to establish leadership before someone else does)
- YES → Go to Q3

**Q3: Can you beat the leader on their own terms (overall market)?**
- YES → Consider "Head to Head" (you need overwhelming differentiation + resources)
- NO → Go to Q4

**Q4: Is there a subsegment with a clear unmet need that you can serve better than the leader?**
- YES → Consider "Big Fish, Small Pond"
- NO → Rethink your positioning or consider if you truly have product-market fit

## Common Traps

### Trap 1: Defining competitive alternatives as any possible "competitor"
The most common mistake. Ask "What would a customer do if we didn't exist?" not "Who could theoretically compete with us?" Don't include ghost competitors. If you start seeing them in deals, adjust positioning then. Until then, you're diluting your positioning.

### Trap 2: Assuming you must create a new category to grow
The allure of "owning" a category is strong, but the reality is harsh. It requires massive investment in education, and first movers often lose to fast followers. Start by positioning in an existing category. Once you have hundreds of millions in revenue, you can consider expanding or redefining category boundaries.

### Trap 3: For startups — thinking your niche is "too small"
"Blue Sea vs Blue Ocean" thinking. You MUST start with focus on a precise niche (CRM for Investment Banks), then move to adjacent targets (CRM for Banks), then broaden further (CRM for Financial Services). This is how great companies are built. The niche is your entry point, not your ceiling.
