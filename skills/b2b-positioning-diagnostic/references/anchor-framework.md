## The Positioning Bet Mindset

One of the biggest GTM mistakes early-stage founders make: they wait for perfect data before positioning. But **positioning creates the data.**

The moment you decide what your product is ("We are X"), who it's for ("We help X"), and who you compete against ("Instead of Z") — everything sharpens: clear ICP targeting, better messaging, more focused sales calls, smarter roadmap decisions, content that converts, competitors you can position against.

Yes, it's a bet. Yes, it might be wrong.

**But: bad positioning beats no positioning. Every time.**

Positioning isn't about being right. It's about having something to test.

**Quick check**: If someone asked you right now "What are you?" — could you answer in one sentence without rambling? If you can't do it in 30 seconds, your buyers won't get it either.

---

## The 3-Step Framework

Positioning is two decisions layered together:

1. **Choose your Primary Anchor** — What are you? (how buyers classify you)
2. **Add a Secondary Angle** — Why you? (your differentiation)
3. **Combine both** into a clear positioning statement

**Strong positioning = Primary Anchor + Secondary Angle**

## Primary Anchors — Comparison Table

There are 4 Primary Anchors. Each answers "What is your product?" in a way that matches how buyers think TODAY.

If buyers can't mentally classify you, they can't buy you. Confused buyers don't ask for demos — they ignore you.

### 1. Activity Positioning

| Dimension | Detail |
|---|---|
| **1-sentence explanation** | You position your product around a task people do manually today, before there is a clearly defined software category for it |
| **How you describe it** | "We help you do X" |
| **Buyer awareness** | Low |
| **Market maturity** | Pre-market |
| **Dominant alternative (status quo)** | Manual workflows |
| **Buying comparison** | Doing nothing |
| **Budget ownership** | No clear budget |
| **Search demand** | None / very low |
| **G2 / Capterra category** | Does not exist |
| **Messaging focus** | Name the activity + expose the pain |
| **Proof type focus** | Before → After transformation (show how painful/manual/inefficient the previous workflow was and how it improves) |
| **Proof formats** | "Before vs. After" stories, time saved comparisons, workflow transformation visuals |
| **Main GTM goal** | Create awareness |
| **Clarity test** | "This is work we do." |

### 2. Use Case Positioning

| Dimension | Detail |
|---|---|
| **1-sentence explanation** | You position your product around a clear job-to-be-done, describing the concrete use case and the friction you remove |
| **How you describe it** | "We help you do X without Y" |
| **Buyer awareness** | Medium |
| **Market maturity** | Emerging |
| **Dominant alternative (status quo)** | Broken workflows and tool stacks |
| **Buying comparison** | Alternative workflows |
| **Budget ownership** | Emerging |
| **Search demand** | Problem / workflow-based |
| **G2 / Capterra category** | Fragmented |
| **Messaging focus** | Job-to-be-done + friction removed |
| **Proof type focus** | Workflow Power (how powerful the new workflow is) + Outcome Proof (how it achieves the job "better") |
| **Proof formats** | Customer stories ("new way is so much better"), workflow demos / product walkthroughs, outcome-focused case studies ("We reduced X by Y%") |
| **Main GTM goal** | Own the use case |
| **Clarity test** | "This is work we want to do better." |

### 3. Product Category Positioning

| Dimension | Detail |
|---|---|
| **1-sentence explanation** | You position your product as a type of software buyers already recognize and actively compare |
| **How you describe it** | "We are a X" |
| **Buyer awareness** | High |
| **Market maturity** | Established |
| **Dominant alternative (status quo)** | Direct competitors |
| **Buying comparison** | Other vendors |
| **Budget ownership** | Clear budget line item |
| **Search demand** | Category-based |
| **G2 / Capterra category** | Clearly defined |
| **Messaging focus** | Differentiation within category |
| **Proof type focus** | Comparative Proof (show why you are better than other category vendors) |
| **Proof formats** | Product comparisons, battlecards, G2/Capterra badges, analyst reports, category rankings, "Top X [Category] Tools" listings, case studies ("Best tool for X") |
| **Main GTM goal** | Win comparisons |
| **Clarity test** | "This is the type of software we need." |

### 4. Competitive Alternative Positioning

| Dimension | Detail |
|---|---|
| **1-sentence explanation** | You position your product as a direct alternative to a well-known market leader or dominant status quo |
| **How you describe it** | "We're a [Leader]-alternative" / "We replace X" |
| **Buyer awareness** | High |
| **Market maturity** | Mature (clear market leader exists) |
| **Dominant alternative (status quo)** | Named market leader (dominant vendor) |
| **Buying comparison** | Leader comparison |
| **Budget ownership** | Budget already allocated |
| **Search demand** | Competitor-based |
| **G2 / Capterra category** | Clearly defined |
| **Messaging focus** | "Why us vs X" |
| **Proof type focus** | Switch & Upgrade Proof (show how customers moved from the leader to you, and what improved) |
| **Proof formats** | Product comparisons, "Switched from [Leader] to us" case studies, ROI comparisons, G2/Capterra badges, "Best Alternative to X" rankings |
| **Main GTM goal** | Steal demand from the leader |
| **Clarity test** | "We currently use X." |

## Decision Tree: How to Choose Your Primary Anchor

**Choose your Primary Anchor based on how buyers think today, not how you want them to think.**

Answer top to bottom. No strategy debates — just yes/no.

### Step 0: Leader Dominance Test (Check First)

**Q0**: Is there a dominant, well-known market leader that almost every buyer already knows, owns most category mindshare, is frequently referenced in conversations, and is the default comparison point?

- **YES** (and you have a clear wedge) → Consider **Competitive Alternative Positioning**
- **NO** → Continue below

**When to use Competitive Alternative**: a dominant leader exists, buyers know that leader, and you have a clear wedge. **Avoid it if**: the leader is not widely known, or you cannot clearly articulate a wedge.

If you choose Competitive Alternative, still validate Category Awareness below.

### Step 1: Category Awareness Test

**Q1**: When buyers start evaluating solutions, do they google a specific software category? Compare vendors on G2 or Capterra? Ask for demos of multiple vendors in the same category?

- **YES** → **Product Category Positioning**
- **NO** → Go to Step 2

### Step 2: Problem Articulation Test

**Q2**: Can buyers clearly describe what they want to achieve, even if they don't know what type of software solves it? (e.g., "We need to schedule interviews faster," "We need better onboarding walkthroughs")

- **YES** → **Use Case Positioning**
- **NO** → Go to Step 3

### Step 3: Manual Behavior Test

**Q3**: Is the dominant solution still manual? Do most potential customers say: "We just use spreadsheets," "We handle this in Slack/email," "We don't really have a tool for this"?

- **YES** → **Activity Positioning**
- **NO** → Re-evaluate your market understanding

## Level of Specificity

Choosing the right anchor is not just about WHICH type. It's also about choosing it at the **right level of specificity.**

- Too vague → Confusing
- Too specific → Too niche / small target audience
- Right level → Clear & defensible

### CRM Market

| Positioning Type | Too Vague | Too Specific | Right Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Activity ("We help you...") | Managing customers | Logging deal stage changes after sales calls | Track all your customer infos in one database |
| Product Category ("We are a...") | Business software | Outbound Sales CRM software | CRM software |

### Product Analytics Market

| Positioning Type | Too Vague | Too Specific | Right Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Use Case ("We help you...") | Improving your product | Track user adoption for newly released features | Understand how users behave inside your product |
| Product Category ("We are a...") | Analytics software | Feature adoption analytics software | Product Analytics Software |

### Digital Adoption Market

| Positioning Type | Too Vague | Too Specific | Right Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Use Case ("We help you...") | Improving onboarding | Guide users through onboarding during free trial signup | Guide users through SaaS product onboarding |
| Product Category ("We are a...") | SaaS tool | In-app tooltip software | Digital Adoption Platform |

## Positioning and Market Maturity

With market maturity, your positioning anchors might "mature" as well:

**Activity → Use Case → Category (→ Alternative)**

But it doesn't always need to be linear. You can deliberately stay in one positioning type even when a category exists. Why?

- The category may be crowded
- Competing in the category is expensive
- Owning a specific use case can be more defensible

## Positioning by Channel & Funnel Stage

Most products don't sit perfectly in one column. Your Primary Anchor defines your strategic foundation, but how you emphasize it can vary by channel and funnel stage:

- **Activity Positioning** in thought leadership and LinkedIn content → to educate the market and highlight manual inefficiencies
- **Use Case Positioning** on your website → to convert buyers who already recognize the job-to-be-done
- **Product Category Positioning** on comparison pages or paid search → to capture high-intent category demand

## Secondary Differentiators

Your primary anchor classifies you. Your secondary anchor **differentiates you** inside the chosen anchor.

What makes you meaningfully different from alternatives? On what dimension do you compete?

Choose max 2 differentiators:

| Differentiator | What It Means | How It Sounds |
|---|---|---|
| **Niche Down** | Focus on a specific ICP | "Built for B2B SaaS founders" |
| **Low-Cost Player** | Compete on affordability | "Without enterprise pricing" |
| **Premium Player** | Compete on performance & depth | "The most advanced way to..." |
| **Unique Attribute** | A specific feature/capability/fact only true for you | "GDPR-compliant alternative to X" / "Only CRM with native WhatsApp integration" |
| **Lite Version** | Simpler alternative to complex tools | "A lightweight way to..." |

You can also **Mix** — combine 2 angles strategically (e.g., "Built for startups, without complexity" = Niche + Lite).

## Common Anchor-Specific Mistakes

These mistakes are specific to the Anchor Framework:

1. **Mixing multiple primary anchors in one statement.** Pick one. One statement, one anchor. You can use different anchors on different channels (see "Positioning by Channel"), but each individual statement should have one clear anchor.

2. **Leading with differentiation before having a clear primary anchor.** If buyers can't classify what you ARE, they won't process why you're DIFFERENT. Primary anchor first, then secondary angle.
