## Single Product = Simple

If you have one product, your product positioning IS your company positioning. Don't create separate positioning for company and product — it's unnecessary complexity. Include all distinct capabilities of the company in the product positioning as reasons for a buyer to choose you.

When (if!) you add product #2, you can decide if you need separate company positioning. Don't over-engineer naming either — keep it simple. (Example: IBM's DB2 division had dozens of products worth billions, all with descriptive names like "DB2 Information Integrator" — boring but crystal clear.)

## Multi-Product Architectures

When you have multiple products, the positioning architecture depends on HOW YOU SELL. Position the way you sell.

### Option 1: Standard / Cascading Multi-Product

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│          Company Positioning                 │
│    (sum of capabilities of all products)     │
├──────────────┬──────────────┬───────────────┤
│  Product A   │  Product B   │  Product C    │
│  Positioning │  Positioning │  Positioning  │
└──────────────┴──────────────┴───────────────┘
```

**When to use**: Company sells each product separately, no obvious bundles or suites. Company positioning is an umbrella encompassing all product capabilities (like IBM: hardware + software + services).

Each product has its own positioning with its own competitive alternatives, differentiated value, and best-fit accounts. Company positioning is the sum of all product capabilities. The sales team can tell both stories — company level and product level — and they must reinforce each other.

### Option 2: Lead Product with Cross-Sell

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│          Company Positioning                 │
│       (= lead product + extras)              │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│          Lead Product Positioning            │
├──────────────────────┬──────────────────────┤
│  Cross-Sell Product  │  Cross-Sell Product   │
│    Positioning       │    Positioning        │
└──────────────────────┴──────────────────────┘
```

**When to use**: You always sell one product FIRST, then cross-sell/upsell others to existing customers.

The lead product has its own positioning for new customers. Cross-sell products ASSUME the customer already owns the lead product. This is powerful because cross-sell products don't need to be the best in the world — just the best for companies already using YOUR lead product.

**Example — Salesforce (pre-Slack)**: CRM (Sales Cloud) was the lead product. Marketing Cloud, Service Cloud, etc. were sold only to teams that already had the CRM. Marketing Cloud's positioning could assume the buyer owned Salesforce CRM. They didn't need to be the world's best marketing platform — just the best for Salesforce CRM users.

### Option 3: Product Family / Platform

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│          Company Positioning                 │
│        (= platform positioning)              │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│          Platform Positioning                │
│  ┌──────────┬──────────┬──────────┐         │
│  │Product A │Product B │Product C │         │
│  └──────────┴──────────┴──────────┘         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**When to use**: You have 2+ products sold to the same company, and you expect companies to buy both (together or one at a time, no particular order). You want buyers to understand the value of buying some or all products from you.

Position products together as a "family" or "platform." Your positioning should reflect your company's point of view about the market: if you believe customers should buy products together, position them together and explain why. Whether customers can purchase separately is a pricing/packaging decision, not a positioning decision.

**Note on "platform"**: Many customers find the term meaningless — interchangeable with "product family" or "suite." Use it carefully.

### Option 4: Different Products for Different Segments

**When to use**: Multiple products sold to completely different types of companies.

Position each product separately. The key question: is there value in having a company position that ties products together under an umbrella?

Sometimes yes — expertise in one market helps sell in another (e.g., a streaming video company whose media company experience built trust with developers, and vice versa).

Sometimes no — companies handle this by forming essentially separate companies under a holding company, each with its own positioning.

### Choosing Your Architecture — Key Questions

1. Do you always lead with one product? → **Lead Product** architecture
2. Can customers choose any product first or buy combinations? → **Platform/Family** architecture
3. Do you sell each product independently to separate markets? → **Standard/Cascading** or **Separate** architecture
4. Do products serve the same buyer or different buyers? → Same buyer suggests Platform; different buyers may require separate positioning

## Multiple Market Segments

**Common scenario**: Your product serves multiple industries (e.g., banks AND insurance companies). Do you need different positioning for each?

**Usually NO.** Different segments rarely need distinct positioning. Here's why:

When you run a positioning exercise, even if competitors differ between segments, the differentiated CAPABILITIES tend to be the same (or similar), so the differentiated VALUE is the same, and therefore the POSITIONING is the same.

Think about it: if the positioning were genuinely different — different competitors, different capabilities, completely different value per segment — you'd actually have a product strategy problem. The features each segment wants and the roadmap needed to stay differentiated would diverge. You'd eventually need to split into two products.

**What you DO customize per segment:**

```
┌────────┐  ┌────────┐  ┌────────┐
│Segment │  │Segment │  │Segment │
│   A    │  │   B    │  │   C    │
└───┬────┘  └───┬────┘  └───┬────┘
    │           │           │
    └───────────┼───────────┘
                │
    ┌───────────┴──────────┐
    │  Common Positioning   │
    └───────────┬──────────┘
          ┌─────┴─────┐
    ┌─────┴──┐   ┌────┴────┐
    │Common  │   │ Common  │
    │Sales   │   │Messaging│
    │Pitch   │   │         │
    │(w/     │   │(w/      │
    │tailored│   │tailored │
    │proof)  │   │content) │
    └────────┘   └─────────┘
```

- **Homepage**: One message that works for all segments
- **Campaigns**: Segment-specific terminology, customer references from that segment
- **Sales deck**: Common core pitch, but swap in segment-specific case studies in the "proof" section
- **Content**: Segment-specific use cases expressed in segment language

The value proposition stays the same — you just express it in ways that resonate with each audience.

## Multiple Personas — Focus on the Champion

In a typical B2B enterprise purchase, 4-12+ people are involved. But they are NOT all equal.

```
         ┌──────────────┐
         │   Economic    │
         │    Buyer      │
         └──────┬───────┘
                │
    ┌───────────┼───────────┐
    │           │           │
┌───┴───┐ ┌────┴────┐ ┌────┴───┐
│  IT   │ │ THE     │ │  End   │
│       │ │CHAMPION │ │ Users  │
└───────┘ │(target  │ └────────┘
          │of your  │
┌─────────┤position-│ ┌────────┐
│ Legal   │ing)     │ │Purchas-│
└─────────┤         │ │ing     │
          └─────────┘ └────────┘
```

### The Champion
- The person tasked with recommending a solution to the economic buyer
- Usually leads initial calls and decides which vendors make the shortlist
- Must get buy-in from all other stakeholders (IT, Legal, Purchasing, End Users)
- Has the power to MAKE a deal happen

### Everyone Else
- Has the power to KILL a deal (but not make it)
- Raises OBJECTIONS, not value concerns
- Common objections: total cost of ownership, ease of deployment, change management, security, compliance

### The Positioning Rule
- You need POSITIONING for the champion (differentiated value that resonates)
- You need OBJECTION HANDLING for everyone else (addressed in sales, not positioning)
- If positioning doesn't resonate with the champion, you never make the shortlist. Game over at step one.

Sometimes you have co-champions (business side + IT side). That's fine — just be clear about who they are. Often, champions look for value that satisfies BOTH sides because they know both must agree.

## Growth: When to Add Products

### Same customer, same buyer
Most natural expansion. You know the user, you know their needs. Much higher success probability than targeting something completely new.

### Same customer, different buyer
Familiar company but different stakeholder. Be cautious — "there were several products we thought belonged to the same buyer that turned out not to be."

### New customer entirely
New ideas can come from entirely new wells. Consider: "Does this unlock a new user base we weren't targeting before?"

### Key questions before expanding
- **Why now?** What makes this something we must build NOW that we can't build later? Is there a unique market timing that makes this urgent?
- **Analytics vs instincts?** The methodical answer (expand markets, new products, capture more TAM, raise prices) gives you singles and doubles. Home runs often come from intuitive calls about where the market is heading.
- **Keep it lean.** Don't overfund new bets. In the early phase of establishing something new, more resources actually hold you back. Fewer people with maximum skills.
