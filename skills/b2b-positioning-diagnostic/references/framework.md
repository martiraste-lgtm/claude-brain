## What Positioning Is

Positioning is context-setting for products. It clarifies your product's role and relevance in a category, identifies a space you can legitimately own in your customer's mind, and serves as the north star guiding product, marketing, and sales efforts.

Good positioning triggers a set of assumptions about your product that are TRUE: who you compete with, what features you should have, who it's for, what it should cost. Bad positioning triggers assumptions that are FALSE, forcing sales and marketing to undo the damage positioning already caused.

Example: Saying "CRM" triggers assumptions about competing with Salesforce, selling to head of sales, having deal/account tracking, and costing less than Salesforce. If those assumptions are wrong, you're fighting uphill from the start.

A shift in positioning can completely transform how people perceive a product — and mean the difference between success and failure.

**Positioning is NOT:**
- A tagline or slogan
- A marketing campaign
- A product strategy (it doesn't set product direction)
- A one-time exercise (it evolves as product, market, and customers change)

**Positioning IS:**
- A strategic decision about how to communicate product value to target customers
- The foundation that messaging, homepage, sales pitch, and content are built upon
- A company problem, not a marketing problem — it shows up in marketing, sales, and customer success but requires leadership alignment

## Pre-Positioning Decisions

Before starting a positioning exercise, four critical decisions must be made. Skipping these causes conflict, rework, and failed exercises.

### Decision 1: Positioning Readiness Check

Is now the right time?

- **Pre-launch product**: What you're creating is a positioning THESIS, not true positioning. You're making educated guesses. Keep positioning loose pre-launch — launch, see where the market pulls you, then tighten based on what you learn.
- **Pre-PMF / very early**: Stay flexible and open to market feedback. You need to discover PATTERNS: who loves your product and WHY. Until you know this, the full 5-step positioning process is premature. **However: "bad positioning beats no positioning."** Even pre-PMF, taking a positioning bet gives you something to test — it sharpens ICP targeting, messaging, sales calls, and roadmap decisions. Positioning creates the data you need to refine it. For early-stage founders, use the Anchor Framework (see `references/anchor-framework.md`) as a faster, lighter positioning exercise. Keep it loose, but have SOMETHING.
- **Post-PMF, ready to scale**: This is the right time for the full 5-step process. You have enough data about best-fit customers, competitive dynamics, and differentiated value to do proper positioning work.
- **Signal that you're ready for the full process**: You can identify your best customers (who buys repeatedly, who recommends you). If you can't identify best-fit customers, use the Anchor Framework to take a positioning bet first, then refine with the 5-step process as you gather data.

### Decision 2: Who Is This Positioning For?

The methodology is for CUSTOMER positioning — not investor, employee, or partner positioning.

- **Customer positioning**: focuses on NOW and immediate future. What do customers get for their money today vs alternatives?
- **Investor positioning**: focuses on FUTURE. Vision, path to becoming market leader.
- **Employee positioning**: focuses on career growth, mission, compensation.

The team must agree to focus on customer positioning. If you don't decide this upfront, half the team will try to make the story work for investors, producing mushy positioning.

### Decision 3: What Exactly Are You Positioning?

- **Single-product company**: Product positioning = Company positioning. Don't overcomplicate it. When you add product #2, you can separate them.
- **Multi-product company**: Decide whether you're positioning a single product, the entire company, a suite/platform, or some combination. This is tied to your go-to-market strategy (see references/multi-product.md).

### Decision 4: Which Persona Are You Positioning For?

Position for the deal CHAMPION — the person who:
- Creates the shortlist
- Ensures the right stakeholders are involved
- Makes recommendations to the economic buyer
- Conducts initial calls with vendors

Your positioning must resonate with the champion. If it doesn't, you never make the shortlist. You never meet other stakeholders. You're dead at step one.

The champion cares about differentiated value. All other stakeholders (IT, Legal, Purchasing, End Users, Economic Buyer) raise OBJECTIONS — which are handled in sales, not in positioning.

## Use Case Map

Before positioning, you need a complete Use Case Map. If any element is missing, do market analysis first.

| Element | Question |
|---------|----------|
| **Problem** | What is the problem, in the user's/customer's own words? |
| **Persona** | Who has this problem? |
| **Alternatives** | What alternatives exist today to solve it? |
| **Frequency** | How often does the user/customer encounter this problem? |
| **Why** | Why does the customer choose our product to solve it? |

**Example — Figma (core product):**
| Element | Answer |
|---------|--------|
| Problem | Product designers work in silos; getting reviews from colleagues (PMs) requires many iterations in a friction-filled process (download, upload to Dropbox, send, get comments via email, etc.) |
| Persona | Product designers of digital products |
| Alternatives | Adobe, Sketch, InVision |
| Frequency | Practically every day |
| Why | Figma is the only purely browser-based design tool that enables easy collaboration between designers and non-designers, speeding up iterations and simplifying the feedback loop |

## The 5-Step Process

Work through these five components IN THIS ORDER. Each step feeds the next.

### Step 1: Competitive Alternatives
**Question**: If you didn't exist, what would customers use?

This is NOT a test of your internet research skills. Focus on what customers ACTUALLY do, not what they theoretically could do. Include:
- Direct competitors customers actually encounter in deals
- Status quo solutions (spreadsheets, manual processes, hiring an intern)
- "Do nothing" — but understand this really means "keep using current approach"

Do NOT include:
- "Ghost competitors" you never actually see in deals
- "Horizon competitors" that might compete someday but don't today
- Every company that could theoretically compete

**Critical insight**: In B2B, vendors typically lose ~50% of deals to status quo ("no decision"). Companies where this exceeds 80% exist. Your positioning must position against the status quo if you want customers to act.

### Step 2: Distinct Capabilities
**Question**: What distinct capabilities do you have that alternatives do not?

List features, attributes, capabilities that are UNIQUE to you. This includes:
- Product features
- Business model advantages
- Relationships or partnerships
- Processes or IP
- Data or network effects

### Step 3: Differentiated Value
**Question**: What value do the distinct capabilities enable for customers?

This is the hardest and most important step. Move from features to value using the "So what?" technique:

Feature → "So what?" → Benefit → "So what?" → VALUE (mapped to customer goal)

Keep asking "So what?" until you reach value the BUYER understands and cares about. Then STOP — going further makes it too abstract ("improve operational efficiency" — every competitor says this).

**In B2B, there are only two pure points of value**: helping businesses make money or save money. (Insurance/risk reduction is a special case.) But don't abstract so far that you lose differentiation.

The UVP must be: **Relevant** (solves an important problem), **Impactful** (high frequency/severity), and **Differentiated** (alternatives can't deliver it).

### Step 4: Best-Fit Accounts
**Question**: Which types of companies care A LOT about that differentiated value?

Many buyers may care about your value, but some care MUCH MORE than others. Identify the characteristics that make a company care deeply:
- Industry or vertical
- Company size or stage
- Specific workflows or tech stack
- Regulatory environment
- Organizational structure

Your best-fit customers are the ones who buy repeatedly, recommend you, and get the most value. They give you the keys to understanding your product's real strengths.

**Warning**: Positioning on special features rather than on customer fit is an ERROR. Feature-based positioning lets customers compare you against the wrong competitors.

### Step 5: Market Category
**Question**: What context makes your differentiated value obvious for these best-fit accounts?

The market category is the frame of reference that makes your value self-evident. Choose from three strategies (see references/market-category.md):
1. **Head to Head**: Compete to win an existing market
2. **Big Fish, Small Pond**: Win a subsegment of an existing market
3. **Create a New Category**: Define a new market

## Feature → Benefit → Value Mapping

| Feature (what your product does/has) | Benefit (what it enables for customers) | Value (how it maps to customer goals) |
|---|---|---|
| 15-megapixel camera | Sharp photo images | Images can be zoomed in or printed large and still look sharp |
| All-metal construction | Stronger frame that resists damage | Frame lasts 5x longer, saving $50K/year on replacements |
| One-click reports | Fast, easy report generation | Every part of the organization can make better decisions based on accurate, up-to-date metrics |
| 24-hour support | Support always available | Global operations have access to help across every time zone |

Each feature can have multiple value points. A COMBINATION of features can also provide value. Group related capabilities into 2-3 "value themes" — don't overwhelm with 10 reasons.

## Differentiation vs. Competitive Advantage

> "The most differentiated product might just be something nobody wants." — Ravi Mehta

Completing the Feature → Benefit → Value mapping is necessary but not sufficient. Before validating a value theme, apply this critical filter: **is this a competitive advantage or just a difference?**

Being unique is not inherently valuable. Uniqueness only matters if customers in your market want the unique thing you're offering — AND if that uniqueness makes them materially better off. Teams routinely confuse "what makes us different?" with "what makes us better?" — and positioning built on the former collapses in sales.

### The operative distinction

| | Difference | Competitive Advantage |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | A capability or attribute unique to you | A difference that makes you materially better for the customer |
| **Customer behavior** | Customer notes it, may appreciate it, doesn't drive decision | Customer actively seeks it, drives purchase and retention |
| **Over time** | Static — others can copy it | Compounding — network effects, data, switching costs amplify it |
| **Example** | Tidal: exclusive tracks, CD-quality audio | Spotify: reliable streaming that works across all conditions |

Tidal had genuine differences. Spotify had the advantage — streaming that actually worked on any connection. Listeners want music uninterrupted more than they care about exclusive tracks they'll likely never search for. Spotify won not by being unique, but by being better at the one thing that mattered most.

Similarly: Uber didn't win because it was different from Lyft. It won because its larger driver network created faster pickups and lower prices — an advantage that compounded over time as the network grew.

### The filter: apply it to every value theme

After completing the "So what?" chain for each capability, run three questions:

**1. "Does this make us materially better for the customer — not just different?"**
If the answer is "we're unique in this" but not "customers are better off because of this," it's distinctiveness, not a positioning asset.

**2. "Do customers actively want this — or are we hoping they will?"**
Uniqueness doesn't create demand. The capability must map to something customers already feel is important. If you have to explain why they should care, it's not a clear advantage.

**3. "Does this advantage compound over time, or can a competitor replicate it in a sprint?"**
The strongest advantages get harder to replicate: network effects (more users → better product), proprietary data (more usage → smarter output), switching costs (deeper integration → higher cost of leaving). A difference that can be copied in 6 months is a short-term edge, not a sustainable positioning foundation.

### Implication for the positioning exercise

Apply this filter before finalizing value themes. Some will survive as genuine advantages — keep them front and center in positioning. Others will reveal themselves as mere distinctions — useful for feature-by-feature comparisons in sales but not load-bearing for the positioning statement.

The goal isn't uniqueness. It's competitiveness — being better in the ways that matter most to best-fit accounts.

## UVP Criteria

The Unique Value Proposition must pass three tests:

1. **Differentiated**: At the time of positioning, alternatives cannot deliver this same value. What makes your approach/capability unique?
2. **Impactful**: The problem occurs frequently enough or is severe enough that solving it creates meaningful impact on the customer's workflow or business.
3. **Relevant**: It solves an important problem for the target persona — a problem they recognize and actively want to solve.

## Templates

### April Dunford's Positioning Template

| UNIQUE ATTRIBUTES | VALUE | WHO CARES (A LOT)? |
|---|---|---|
| Features or attributes (business model, relationships, processes, IP) that are unique to us | The benefit the attribute (or combination) enables for customers | Characteristics of a customer that makes them particularly excited about that value. How can we identify them? |

**MARKET CATEGORY FRAME OF REFERENCE**: The market category that makes our value most obvious to the prospects that care the most.

### Startup Positioning Template

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| **What is it** | Shortest possible statement of what you are |
| **Target Segment** | The specific target market you are targeting in the short term |
| **Market Category** | The market that you compete in |
| **Competitive Alternatives** | If your customers don't use you, what do they use? |
| **Primary Differentiation** | The one thing that sets you apart most from competitive alternatives |
| **Key Benefit** | The biggest benefit your target market derives from your offering |

### Positioning Statement Format

For **[WHO]** who need to **[WHY]**, **[PRODUCT]** provides **[WHAT]**. Unlike other solutions, it **[HOW / DIFFERENTIATOR]**, thereby **[KEY BENEFIT]**.

**Example — Figma**: For Product Designers who need to improve collaboration among them and across teams, Figma provides the first browser-based design platform. Unlike other solutions, it allows designers to collaborate in real time and bring anyone into the design process, thereby breaking silos.

## Positioning vs Messaging

| POSITIONING | MESSAGING |
|---|---|
| A strategic decision about how to communicate product value to target customers | Customer-facing language used to bring the positioning to life |

**Example — Figma:**
- **Positioning**: "For Product Designers who need to improve collaboration among them and across teams, Figma provides the first browser-based design platform..."
- **Messaging**: "Nothing great is made alone. Figma connects everyone in the design process so teams can deliver better products, faster."

Positioning comes first. Messaging translates it into language that resonates emotionally. A brilliant message built on weak positioning amplifies confusion.

## PLG Messaging Sequencing

The principle of "right message at right moment" (message-moment fit) applies to both PLG and SLG. The sequence:

| Stage | Customer Situation | Goal | Lead With | SLG Channel |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Awareness** | Not using product, not aware | Get them to consider | Problem & Use Case | Homepage, content, outreach |
| **Acquisition** | Just became aware | Get them to try/engage | Capabilities | First call, demo intro |
| **Activation** | Signed up / first meeting | Get to first "Aha Moment" | Features (guided) | Detailed demo, POC |
| **Habit** | Using core flow | Actualize value (repeated use) | Benefits with proof | Follow-up, case studies |
| **Expansion** | Regular user/customer | Additional "Aha Moments" | Adjacent Capabilities | Upsell conversations |
| **Monetization** | Experienced repeated value | Pay for scaled value | Scaled Capabilities & Price | Proposal, negotiation |

In PLG, messages live IN the product. In SLG, messages live in sales conversations, decks, emails, and content. The sequencing logic is identical — only the delivery vehicle changes.
