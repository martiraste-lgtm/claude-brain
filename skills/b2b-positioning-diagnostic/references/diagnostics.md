## Is It a Positioning Problem?

Before doing positioning work, verify the problem is actually about positioning. Ask these diagnostic questions internally:

1. Walk around the company and ask each person: "Describe the problem we solve." Are answers aligned?
2. Ask: "How would you describe our startup to someone who's never heard of it?" Do the elevator pitches match in essence, not just phrasing?
3. Ask: "Define our ICP and Buyer Persona." Is there agreement?
4. Ask: "Do product priorities come from strategic choices or from what's easy to build today?"

If answers diverge significantly, it's a positioning problem.

**Positioning problem signal**: Leads enter the pipeline but don't understand what you do, compare you to wrong competitors, or can't see why they need you.

**Marketing/demand gen problem signal**: Every time you talk to a customer you convert them, but you can't find enough of them. The issue is reaching best-fit customers more often, not positioning.

## Positioning Problem vs Marketing Problem

This diagnostic table helps distinguish real positioning problems from marketing/sales issues they're commonly confused with.

| SYMPTOM | TRUE CAUSE (Positioning) | COMMONLY CONFUSED WITH (Marketing/Sales) |
|---|---|---|
| Long sales cycles | Lack of focus on UVP and ICP | "Sales can't close" / "Demo is confusing" / "Sales deck is weak" |
| Users don't recognize product value | Too many features, none solving a specific problem completely | "Messaging & copy is ineffective" / "Need more emoji in emails" |
| Very low repurchase rate | Superficial TA segmentation and over-promising during sales | "Wrong remarketing tactics" / "Send another newsletter with 20% discount" |
| Low product usage | Product doesn't meet needs or expectations effectively | "UX/UI problems" / "Gamified onboarding will fix it" |
| Low conversion rate | Unclear UVP and differentiating element | "Price is too high" / "Competitors have one more feature, we need it too" |
| Ineffective marketing messages | Poor knowledge of Target Audience and Competitors | "Homepage/Landing/Content is confusing" / "We need a genius CLAIM!" |
| Losing deals to inferior alternatives | Lack of clarity on differentiating element vs alternatives | "Price too high" / "Not enough advertising" / "Let's do an alignment call" |
| Long meetings debating feature priorities | Lack of Vision and clarity on TA problems/needs/desires | "Inefficient product management procedures" / "Need a prioritization framework" |
| Many leads, few ideal customers | Positioning too broad or too generic | "Lead qualification problems" / "Targeting issues" |
| Sales team always asks for discounts | Lack of perceived value, weak differentiation | "Price too high" / "We need a total rebrand, starting from the logo" |

**The core insight**: Marketing AMPLIFIES — it doesn't create clarity. If you haven't clearly chosen who you're for, what you compete against, and why you're preferable in THAT context, every euro spent on marketing increases noise, not traction.

## Hidden Symptoms of Weak Positioning

These symptoms don't announce themselves as positioning problems, which is why they're dangerous:

### "The problem of talking about the problem"
When you ask team members "describe the problem you solve," they talk about the product/solution instead. This is "inside-out thinking" (starting with product) vs "outside-in thinking" (starting with customer).

### "Elevator pitch du jour"
Each team member describes the company with different essence — not just different phrasing. "A more professional, simpler Zoom" is fundamentally different from "an all-inclusive webinar solution." Same domain, completely different value propositions.

### "Product prioritization battleground"
Prioritization meetings become 3-hour debates. Sales says they lost deals because of missing features. The roadmap gets constantly reshuffled. Features launch without depth. The team is in "launch-and-run" mode.

### "Can't agree on ICP"
Sales pursues a different ICP than what product is building for. The decision maker isn't the power user. Without alignment, product and GTM pull in different directions.

### "Losing to worse products"
You're told: "We're not ranking for the right keywords," "Our decks don't tell a good story," "Customers think we do X when we actually do Y," "We keep changing the homepage message." Founders hire a growth marketer or agency, spend money, nothing changes in 3 months.

### "It's for everyone = it's for no one"
When the product is for everyone, it's for no one. Even Notion (used by everyone in an org) focused early on product teams. Datadog focused on infrastructure teams. You can't be all things to everyone, but you can be something great for someone.

### "Constantly changing homepage message"
If you're reshuffling homepage copy every few weeks, you don't have a messaging problem — you have a positioning problem. The message keeps changing because the strategic foundation underneath is unclear.

## Weak Positioning: Symptoms and Causes

| Symptom | Potential Causes |
|---|---|
| Prospects still don't get what you do | Market category not well known to prospects. Your value is weak. Your unique differentiators aren't that unique/different. |
| Prospects think you are not "for them" | Target segment is too broad. You haven't explicitly signaled who you target. |
| Prospects don't understand your value | Your value is weak or not as valuable to prospects as you assumed. |
| Prospects don't believe you can deliver | You have no proof you can deliver the value. Your value is too subjective. |
| Prospects think you are like other solutions | Your key unique attributes aren't unique. Your differentiation is weak. |

## The 4 Roadblocks

These map to specific steps in the positioning process:

### Roadblock 1: Disagreement about what to position AGAINST
**Where it hits**: Step 1 (Competitive Alternatives)

Marketing, Product, Sales, and Founders each have different views of competition. This disagreement is the root of most weak positioning.

**Solution — Focus on the prospect's view:**
1. Imagine the prospect's world: "If we didn't exist, what would they do?" Focus on real deals, not theory.
2. Stay rooted in the near-term: Position for the product as it exists today, in the market as it is today. Expect positioning to evolve.
3. Don't forget the status quo: ~50% of B2B deals are lost to "no decision." Understand status quo strengths AND weaknesses.
4. Work with a cross-functional team: Include heads of marketing, sales, product management, customer success, plus the founder/CEO. Add experienced account executives from sales.

### Roadblock 2: Pessimism blinds the team to product strengths
**Where it hits**: Step 2 (Distinct Capabilities)

Teams convince themselves their product is undifferentiated despite winning deals. This mindset is contagious — spreading to sales, who lose confidence.

**Solution — Keep coming back to what's working:**
1. Focus on strengths and near-term competitive advantage. Positioning is about why we win, not where we fall short.
2. Include experienced sales voices — they understand what customers actually value.
3. Prepare the moderator to challenge pessimistic participants with evidence. Customers spend months evaluating and choose YOUR product — they can't all be careless shoppers.

### Roadblock 3: Differentiated value is poorly defined
**Where it hits**: Step 3 (Differentiated Value)

Teams focus on features and assume buyers understand why they matter. The most differentiated capabilities are often the hardest for prospects to understand, precisely because they're uncommon.

**Solution — Home in on the "so what?":**
1. Answer "So what?" / "Why does a customer care?" to go from features to value.
2. Keep asking until you have value the buyer understands — then STOP. Going too far makes it generic.
3. Stay focused on the champion's perspective on value.
4. Remove objections from value themes. Value = reason to buy. Objection = reason NOT to buy (handled separately in sales).

### Roadblock 4: Company doesn't know what they are positioning
**Where it hits**: BEFORE the process starts

Single-product companies trying to create separate company and product positioning (unnecessary). Multi-product companies confused about what to position and in what order.

**Solution — Position the way you sell:**
1. One product = one position in the market. Don't create separate company positioning.
2. Multi-product: let go-to-market strategy drive positioning decisions (see references/multi-product.md).

## Bias Patterns by Function

Each function in a B2B company has predictable biases about competition:

**Marketing**: Overly influenced by competitor marketing spend and visibility. Equates market presence with competitive success — even if sales never encounters that competitor in deals.

**Product Management**: Focused further into the future than marketing/sales. Lists "horizon competitors" (products that aren't causing pain today but might in the future). In positioning, product teams often list twice as many competitors as sales, many never seen on a prospect's shortlist.

**Sales**: Two distinct biases. First, "whale bias" — losing one big deal makes them overly focused on that winning competitor, even if it never appears in normal deals. Second, rarely classifying status quo as a "competitor" — deals lost to status quo are categorized as "no decision," and "No" is mentally translated to "Not yet."

**Founders**: Great gut feel for positioning, but three situations muddy the waters:
- Founder was once heavily involved in closing deals but became less so — biased toward outdated competitive landscape
- Recent acquisitions opened opportunities the acquiring team (and founder) may not fully understand
- Founder has been focused on fundraising — oriented toward future state that sales can't sell today

## Pessimism Patterns

Four ways pessimism manifests and undermines positioning:

1. **Defining ideal customers so broadly that every lost deal seems like product failure.** Product teams get pulled into saving lost-cause deals with bad-fit prospects while good-fit deals close easily without PM involvement. Result: skewed idea of what a good prospect looks like.

2. **Maintaining long hypothetical competitor lists.** Hard to differentiate against a list of 20 competitors you know little about. For roadmap work, track future competitors. For positioning, stay in the reality of the market.

3. **Dismissing sales team insights about why customers buy.** Product team insists prospects "failed to evaluate properly" or sales wins through "trickery" — even when evidence shows clear product superiority in specific areas.

4. **Viewing product management as "problem identification" rather than architecting differentiated value.** Companies get trapped in competitive catch-up cycles, becoming adept at pointing out problems and less attuned to where the product leads. Often, a big differentiator is hiding in plain sight — product takes it for granted while sales sells the heck out of it.

## Differentiated Value Traps

Five ways teams get stuck defining differentiated value:

1. **Assuming prospects understand why a feature is important.** The more unique a feature, the less likely prospects understand its value. A 50-megapixel camera needs no explanation today; 20 years ago, you'd have to explain what a megapixel is and why more is better.

2. **Stopping short of value the prospect actually understands.** "1,000x faster query execution" sounds valuable but customers running monthly reports don't care. The REAL value was "responding to customers 70% faster" — but only for companies using queries for customer support.

3. **Going too far and losing differentiation completely.** In B2B, there are only two pure value points: make money or save money. If you abstract to "this product saves you money," you sound like every competitor.

4. **Too many differentiated value themes.** If you give prospects 10 reasons to pick you, they won't connect with a clear story or remember more than 2-3 (if you're lucky).

5. **Confusing value with objection handling.** Value = reason to BUY. Objection = concern that might prevent buying EVEN IF they want the product. Example: "Easy for IT to manage" might be an objection handler for IT stakeholders, not a value theme for the business buyer champion.

6. **Mistaking uniqueness for advantage.** The team identifies genuine differences from competitors but doesn't verify whether those differences make customers materially better off. A capability can be completely unique and completely irrelevant — uniqueness is not a virtue on its own. Signal: customers acknowledge the difference in demos ("that's interesting") but don't cite it as a reason to buy, don't seek it out, and churn at the same rate as customers without it. Fix: for every value theme, apply the filter "does this difference create a real advantage — or just distinctiveness?" If the customer isn't materially better off with it, it's not a positioning asset regardless of how unique it is. Reference: see the Differentiation vs. Competitive Advantage section in framework.md for the full filter and operative distinction.

## Stage-Specific Problems

### Early-stage / Pre-PMF
- You have a POSITIONING THESIS, not real positioning
- Keep positioning loose — discover where the market pulls you
- Don't over-tighten too soon (launching only for "insurance companies" means only insurance companies try it — you learn nothing else)
- Focus on discovering PATTERNS: who loves the product and WHY

### Post-PMF / Some traction
- **Don't anchor too deeply in your wedge.** Your wedge is an entry point, not your market. Once customers categorize you there, it's very hard to get recategorized.
- Symptoms of wedge-lock: product could serve different use cases but broader adoption isn't happening; confusion about roadmap direction; expansion challenges; competing against wrong competitors.
- **Don't confuse positioning problems with marketing problems** (see diagnostic table above).
- This is the right time for proper positioning work to fuel marketing and sales.

### Established companies
- Usually face the challenge of recognizing that the market has changed. First signals: customer confusion in sales meetings, new competitors causing problems.
- Companies don't change until something bad happens: revenue drops, losing big deals, repeated losses to unknown competitors.
- Execution challenge: in a startup you convince 2 people (CMO + founder); in a big company you have many stakeholders. Must build a cross-functional team with senior managers.
- Must work on brand architecture BEFORE product positioning if multi-product (see references/multi-product.md).

### When to reposition
- Positioning in tech is not forever — it should evolve as you grow toward your vision.
- Triggers: a new feature changes the value you deliver, an acquisition changes product capabilities, market shifts, new competitive dynamics.
- Plan positioning evolution in steps (CRM for Investment Banks → CRM for Banks → CRM for Financial Services → CRM for all).

### Risk perception
The differentiated value must be strong enough to overcome:
- **Inertia of status quo**: "What we have is good enough and comes from a respected company"
- **Startup risk perception**: "It's risky to bet on a startup vs a large established player"
- **The 44% no-decision problem**: 44% of B2B deals end with no decision because the customer decided status quo was safer

Counter risk perception with:
- **Proof of value**: Case studies, third-party validation, anything proving you deliver what you promise
- **Land and expand**: Sell something small first that demonstrably delivers value, then expand
- **PLG / hands-on onboarding**: Let them try the product, or send technical staff to set up the implementation
