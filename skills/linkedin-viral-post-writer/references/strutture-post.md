# LinkedIn Post Structure Examples & Analysis

This reference provides detailed examples of high-performing post structures and analysis of what makes them effective.

## Post Anatomy: The Three-Act Structure

### Act 1: The Hook (First 3 lines)
**Purpose**: Stop the scroll, create curiosity
**Length**: Up to ~174 characters
**Key elements**: Specificity, emotion, value proposition

### Act 2: The Body (Main content)
**Purpose**: Deliver promised value, maintain engagement
**Structure options**:
- Story with lessons
- List with explanations
- Case study breakdown
- Data analysis with insights
- Personal experience with takeaways

### Act 3: The Close (Final paragraph)
**Purpose**: Reinforce value, create call-to-action (if appropriate)
**Options**:
- Summary of key points
- Actionable next step
- Question for engagement
- Emotional resonance
- CTA (for bottom of funnel content)

## Top of Funnel (ToF) Post Example

### Structure Template
```
[HOOK: Broad appeal, trending topic]

[STORY/CONTEXT: Relatable situation or data point]

[KEY INSIGHT #1 with specific example]

[KEY INSIGHT #2 with specific example]

[KEY INSIGHT #3 with specific example]

[OPTIONAL: Additional insights]

[CLOSE: Memorable takeaway or question]
```

### Example ToF Post
**Topic**: Productivity system
**Funnel**: Top (broad appeal)

```
I tracked my productivity for 90 days. The results shocked me.

Here's what actually moves the needle:

Most "productivity advice" is noise. We're told to wake up at 5am,
do cold plunges, and journal for an hour.

I tested it all. Only 3 things actually made a difference:

1. Time blocking (not to-do lists)
Instead of checking tasks off, I blocked specific hours for specific work.
My deep work time tripled from 2 hours to 6 hours per day.

2. Single-tasking (not multitasking)
One browser tab. One document. One meeting at a time.
My output quality improved by 40% according to peer reviews.

3. Energy management (not time management)
I scheduled hard problems for my peak hours (10am-12pm).
Then I batched easy tasks for low-energy windows (2-3pm).

The result?
- 50% more output
- 30% fewer working hours
- Actually enjoying my work again

The counterintuitive part? I do LESS now than before.

What's your biggest productivity struggle?
```

**Why it works**:
- Hook: Personal experiment + surprising results
- Specific numbers throughout
- Contrarian take (do less, not more)
- Actionable insights
- Relatable problem
- Engagement question at end

## Middle of Funnel (MoF) Post Example

### Structure Template
```
[HOOK: Industry-specific or niche topic]

[ESTABLISH AUTHORITY: Your experience/credentials]

[PROBLEM: What most people get wrong]

[SOLUTION: Your framework/approach]
  - Component 1 with specific example
  - Component 2 with specific example
  - Component 3 with specific example

[RESULTS: What this achieves]

[CLOSE: Additional resource or next step]
```

### Example MoF Post
**Topic**: Product management strategy
**Funnel**: Middle (builds authority)

```
After launching 50+ products, I've seen the same mistake kill good ideas.

Most PMs think they need MORE data before shipping.

They're wrong. Here's what actually matters:

The "Minimum Confidence Threshold" framework:

Instead of perfect data, ask 3 questions:
1. Can we fix it fast if we're wrong? (Reversibility)
2. What's the cost of waiting? (Opportunity cost)
3. What's the learning value? (Discovery potential)

Real example from our last launch:
- Data said: Maybe 60% confidence on feature value
- Traditional approach: Run more tests (3-4 weeks)
- Our approach: Ship with kill switch (2 days)

Result: Feature drove 25% engagement lift. We learned more in 48 hours
than we would have in a month of testing.

The counterintuitive insight?
Speed compounds. Each fast iteration teaches you something that makes
the next decision better.

Most PMs over-index on being right.
Great PMs over-index on learning fast.

Want the full framework? It's in my newsletter (link in comments).
```

**Why it works**:
- Hook: Experience + specific problem
- Framework has a name (memorable)
- Real example with numbers
- Shows actual results
- Offers additional resource (captures value)
- Positions authority without being salesy

## Story-Driven Post Structure

### The Narrative Arc Template
```
[HOOK: Intriguing story setup]

[ACT 1: The Challenge]
- Specific situation
- Concrete details
- Emotional element

[ACT 2: The Turning Point]
- What changed
- Specific action taken
- Unexpected element

[ACT 3: The Resolution]
- Results achieved
- Lesson learned
- Broader application

[CLOSE: Universal takeaway]
```

### Example Story Post
```
The intern just corrected the CEO in front of 50 people.

The room went silent. Everyone expected her to get fired on the spot.

Instead, what happened next changed our entire company culture.

Context: We were in our quarterly all-hands. The CEO was presenting
our growth metrics, celebrating hitting our revenue target.

Then Sarah, a 3-week-old intern, raised her hand:
"Those numbers don't account for the refund spike in Q2. The actual
net revenue is 15% lower."

Everyone froze.

The CEO paused. Looked at the slide. Looked at Sarah.

Then said: "You're absolutely right. Thank you for catching that.
Everyone, this is what I want to see more of."

He scrapped the rest of his prepared slides and turned the meeting
into a workshop about our refund problem.

The outcome:
- We fixed a major product issue within 2 weeks
- Refund rate dropped 60% the next quarter
- Sarah got promoted to the core product team
- "Call it like you see it" became our unofficial motto

The lesson isn't about being right.

It's about creating environments where being right doesn't require
being senior.

Most companies say they want honest feedback.
Few reward it when it's uncomfortable.

The best companies do both.
```

**Why it works**:
- Opens with specific, dramatic moment
- Concrete details (names, numbers, dialogue)
- Unexpected resolution (didn't get fired)
- Clear business outcome
- Universal lesson applicable to readers
- Emotional journey throughout

## Data-Driven Post Structure

### The Analysis Template
```
[HOOK: Surprising finding from data]

[METHODOLOGY: How you researched]

[FINDING 1: Pattern + specific examples]

[FINDING 2: Pattern + specific examples]

[FINDING 3: Pattern + specific examples]

[COUNTERINTUITIVE INSIGHT: What this really means]

[CLOSE: Actionable takeaway]
```

### Example Data Post
```
I analyzed 1,000 viral LinkedIn posts. 3 patterns explained 80% of the results.

Methodology: Used a scraper to pull the top 1K posts from Q3 2024
(5M+ impressions each). Analyzed hook structure, length, topic, format.

Here's what actually drives virality:

Pattern 1: Specificity wins
The average viral post had 3.2x more numbers than average posts.
- "grew my business" → 47 shares avg
- "grew from $50K to $500K in 8 months" → 423 shares avg

Pattern 2: Longer hooks perform better
Conventional wisdom says keep hooks short. The data disagrees.
- 1 line hooks: 2,100 avg impressions
- 2 line hooks: 4,800 avg impressions
- 3 line hooks: 8,900 avg impressions

Pattern 3: Emotion + Information combo
Posts with BOTH emotional hooks AND data in the body performed 4.7x
better than posts with only one.

The counterintuitive insight?

"Engagement bait" (asking questions, polls, hot takes) represented
only 2% of viral posts. Value creation dominated at 87%.

What does this mean for you?

Focus on:
1. Adding specific numbers to your stories
2. Using all available hook space (3 lines)
3. Combining emotional hooks with informational value

Test this on your next post and track the difference.
```

**Why it works**:
- Large sample size establishes credibility
- Specific findings with supporting data
- Challenges conventional wisdom
- Each insight is actionable
- Clear methodology shown
- Numbers throughout
- Practical application at end

## Formatting Best Practices

### Line Breaking
**Do**: Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences)
```
This is easier to read.

White space guides the eye.

It creates natural rhythm.
```

**Don't**: Walls of text

### Emphasis Techniques

**Bold sparingly** - Only for key takeaways

• Bullet points for lists (not for prose)
• When listing items or steps
• Creates scannable structure

Numbers are naturally emphatic:
- 47% improvement
- $500K in revenue
- 3 key principles

### Emoji Usage

**Guidelines**:
- Use only if authentic to voice
- Max 1-2 per post for emphasis
- Never in hooks
- Appropriate: 🚀 💡 ⚡ 🎯
- Avoid: 😂 🤣 (too casual) 🔥 (overused)

## Content Quality Checklist

Before posting, verify:
- [ ] Hook creates genuine curiosity (not clickbait)
- [ ] At least 3 specific details (names, numbers, quotes)
- [ ] Clear value proposition (what reader gains)
- [ ] Personal voice (not AI generic)
- [ ] Proper line breaks and white space
- [ ] No obvious typos or errors
- [ ] Appropriate length (not too short, not too long)
- [ ] Funnel type aligns with posting ratio
- [ ] Topic is validated or has proven interest
- [ ] Attribution/credits included (if applicable)

## Length Guidelines by Content Type

**ToF Content**: 150-250 words
- Broad appeal needs quick value delivery
- Mobile-friendly length
- Easy to consume and share

**MoF Content**: 200-350 words
- More depth needed to build authority
- Room for frameworks and examples
- Still digestible in one scroll

**BoF Content**: 250-400 words
- Needs more space for social proof
- Room for detailed offering
- Longer CTAs acceptable

**Story Content**: 300-500 words
- Narrative needs space to develop
- Emotional arcs take time
- Worth the length if engaging

**Exception**: Exceptional content can be any length. Quality trumps arbitrary limits.
